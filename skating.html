Skating

A blog exploring the psychology of inline skating.

I am the perpetual motion engine. For a moment in time, my skates work in a different way to other people's reality. Yesterday evening, 11 June 2026, I skated at the local rink for about 3 hours, with about a 15-minute break throughout the session. When I skate for long periods of time, I find it often cleanses my mind. It is a way to escape the pain of life and to be in a zone where no negative emotions exist, even for a brief moment of time. The first part of the session was very chill; I really got to stretch my legs. Mostly skaters I know were skating in the early session, so I felt pretty comfortable. I love how I can just get a smooth flow going straight away, just pretending that my skates are a perpetual motion engine that never stops. Trying to play games with my own personal reality and change the rules of my universe for a mere moment is an idea that I always come back to. From my perspective, my mental state was normal for skating, with 80 to 100% of my mental load dedicated to situational awareness and technique just based on muscle memory—with a few very minor balance adjustments, or pushing through exhaustion. But just flowing without focusing on technique, and just intuitively pushing out the power transfer, always trying to make the flow as pure and optimal as possible. During the first half of the session, the rink direction was reversed for a brief moment (but it felt like a long time), moving from anticlockwise to clockwise. This period of the session was very insightful. My vision actually works much better in the traditional direction due to vision issues in my right eye; however, I know with practice I can adapt, just like I do with all other areas of my life. My parallel turns are much stronger in the clockwise direction, so I was able to pump around the corners, which was really smooth. But the crossovers are a different story. My crossovers are at 100% on my strong side, but about 10% on my weak side. Interestingly, they went well on my weak side at the local carpark, but in the larger rink, the mechanics were just about there, but none of the power or the flow. That is something that will come with more time and practice. That's always the secret for me—it's the number of hours on my skates—that's it. Switching back to the "normal" direction, my crossovers felt so flexible; it was a fascinating experience, and very valuable and insightful. I decided to stay into the next session, as I cannot ignore a chill rink—it always seems to call to me. In this next session, it was also very quiet. There was one skater who was extremely distracted as they were talking on a phone/headset. In a public session, there is nothing more unpredictable than a distracted skater; it does not matter if they are a beginner, an intermediate, or a pro—it makes no difference. But I always work hard to breathe deeply and create empathy for everyone on the rink because everyone is there for their own reasons—some just to have a fun night, some to post to social media, some to skate while they talk on the phone. Personally, I will never understand fully why people do not focus 100% of the time like my mind does. Because that specific skater was so hard to predict and their behavior so erratic, it was like a fun game to decode and predict their unpredictable movements. I ended up often cutting corners as it was just too risky to get close. The skater would often drift between all the lines (inside, middle, and outside). Optimally, I'd always want to pass via the outside line, however, there was simply no opportunity available because of the constant drifting. I also learned that staying behind the skater and simply waiting did not produce any nice opportunities for passing; it actually made things worse as I was exposed to the erratic behavior for longer periods of time. So, it was better just to choose a safe, risk-averse pass and move on. The lesson is that even on a quiet rink, one distracted skater can make things very challenging as I have to mentally work so hard on two main fronts: first, flag them mentally as a high priority and actively problem-solve each time I pass them; and second, track them at all times as they will do things like stand in place, stand on the wall while on the phone with their skates hanging out on the rink line, or even skate in the wrong direction. It's especially at times when skaters have no momentum on the rink, or when they are skating in the wrong direction, that it takes that extra second to do the environmental tracking to pick them up, because your mind is expecting certain things and what you see them doing makes no sense in the first few milliseconds. I did get frustrated at one moment when the skater completely drifted in front of me on a corner and did not complete the actual turn, but after that, I just took a few deep breaths, suppressed my rising feeling of ego, and really focused on finding empathy for them. Once I can take it from an empathetic mindset and let go of any ego, I can see the skater as a puzzle or a learning opportunity for me to become a better skater in that moment. I think: thank you for teaching me through your "distracted" style of skating. On the flip side, there was another skater in the session who was heavily distracted in a previous session (also on the phone while skating!), but in an amazing turn of events, they were very focused in this new session. From previously analyzing their skating technique, I just knew if they put that phone away I would see their true potential, and that's exactly what happened. It was a Christmas miracle. Just predictable, good skating with a few beginner quirks thrown in for good measure. Pretty good! As I approached the third hour of skating, the only reason I stopped was because my body was getting sore. I'm exhausted today, but I would never trade that experience for the world. And I would do it every day of my life if I could find a way.
